# Spatial Distribution of Skills and Regional Trade Integration
## Candau & Dienesch (2015), The Annals of Regional Science, 54(2), 451–488

### What is this paper about?
This paper asks whether regional trade costs explain the uneven distribution of human capital between regions. It combines a New Economic Geography (NEG) theoretical model with heterogeneous workers, housing, and education investment, and an empirical analysis of the United States from 1940 to 2007.

The key finding is that regional trade integration produces a sorting of workers: high-skilled workers agglomerate in the core while intermediate-skilled workers move to the periphery due to rising housing costs. This sorting then influences the opportunity cost of investing in education at the periphery. The theoretical model predicts a bell-shaped curve: first regional divergence in education investment during trade integration, then convergence at low trade costs. The empirical analysis on U.S. data confirms that regional trade integration has been a determinant of the spatial distribution of skills.

### Important context
- This paper is a NEG paper with heterogeneous agents. It extends Okubo (2010) by adding housing (which drives the sorting mechanism via land prices) and education investment (following Redding and Schott, 2004).
- The sorting result differs from factor-endowment explanations (Heckscher-Ohlin / Michaels, 2008): here it is pecuniary externalities from market size and scale economies, not factor abundance, that drive the spatial distribution of skills.
- The bell-shaped curve of spatial development found in standard NEG masks a more complex sorting pattern: the activities that remain in the core during the dispersion phase are not the same in terms of productivity and skills as those that move to the periphery.
- Housing is critical: high-skilled workers stay in the core because agglomeration benefits outweigh high housing costs for them, while intermediate-skilled workers are priced out.

### Data and methods
- **Historical period (1940–1960)**: Truck registrations as a proxy for domestic trade integration of each U.S. state. Skill distribution measured by educational attainment from Census data.
- **Contemporary period (1997, 2002, 2007)**: U.S. Commodity Flow Survey (CFS) providing inter- and intrastate trade flows. A multidimensional trade cost indicator is constructed including internal, interregional, and international components.
- **Theoretical model**: NEG model with monopolistic competition, increasing returns, iceberg trade costs, heterogeneous workers (high-skilled, intermediate-skilled, unskilled), housing market, and endogenous education investment.
- **Empirical strategy**: Regression of skill distribution on trade integration measures, controlling for state characteristics.

### Key results
1. Regional trade integration produces spatial sorting: high-skilled workers agglomerate in the core, intermediate-skilled workers move to the periphery due to housing costs.
2. This sorting influences education investment: first regional divergence (more education in the core), then convergence at low trade costs as opportunities for local entrepreneurs increase in the periphery.
3. Empirically, truck registrations (1940–1960) and CFS-based trade costs (1997–2007) significantly explain the spatial distribution of skills in U.S. states.
4. Internal trade accounts for about 70% of the weight of all trade in the CFS (2007), confirming that regional trade costs matter.
5. Policy implication: education at the periphery may be maintained during trade integration, because convergence arises for deeper integration.

### Limitations and scope
- The theoretical model is stylized (two regions, specific functional forms) and does not yield fully analytical results for all configurations.
- The empirical analysis uses state-level data, not individual-level migration data. The causal identification relies on the exogeneity of trade cost proxies (truck registrations, CFS flows) which could be challenged.
- The paper focuses on pecuniary externalities (market size, trade costs) and does not model other agglomeration forces such as knowledge spillovers, matching, or amenities that also drive spatial sorting.
- Technological change, which has been critical for the reinvention of some regions, is not modeled — the analysis is essentially static.
